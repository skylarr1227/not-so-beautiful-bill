Division A: Tax Reform

Overview: Comprehensive changes to the federal tax code, including rate extensions, expanded credits, new exemptions, and savings initiatives.

  • Extension of 2017 Tax Cuts:
    Legislative: “The amendments made by this section shall apply to taxable years beginning after December 31, 2025.”
    Effect: Continues reduced tax rates from the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act beyond original sunset dates.
  • Child Tax Credit Increase:
    Legislative: “Section 24(h)(2) is amended by striking ‘$2,000’ and inserting ‘$2,500’.”
    Effect: Increases child tax credit to $2,500 through 2028, then reverts.
  • SALT Deduction Cap Adjustment:
    Legislative: “Section 164(b)(6)(B) is amended by striking ‘$10,000’ and inserting ‘$40,000’.”
    Effect: Raises deduction cap to $40,000 for taxpayers under $500,000 income.
  • No Tax on Tips and Overtime:
    Legislative: “Gross income shall not include any tips or overtime compensation received by the taxpayer.”
    Effect: Excludes tips and overtime from taxable income, reducing tax burdens for hourly workers.
  • MAGA Savings Accounts:
    Legislative: “There is hereby established a program to provide a $1,000 deposit into a savings account for each child born in the United States from 2024 through 2028.”
    Effect: Creates government-funded child savings accounts to promote long-term financial investment.

⚖️ Constitutional & Legal Considerations:

  • Revenue Impact: Extensive cuts may reduce federal income, increasing the deficit.
  • Equity Concerns: Expanded deductions and credits may benefit upper-income brackets more than low-income families.

Division B: Health Care Reform

Overview: Introduces structural reforms to Medicaid, focusing on work requirements, funding restrictions, and enhanced eligibility verification.

  • Medicaid Work Requirements:
    Legislative: “Section 1902(a) is amended to require that, as a condition of eligibility, individuals must engage in 80 hours per month of qualifying activities.”
    Effect: Amends the Social Security Act to mandate work/community engagement for Medicaid recipients, potentially reducing enrollment.
  • Coverage Restrictions:
    Legislative: “No federal funds shall be used to provide gender-affirming care or abortion services under Medicaid.”
    Effect: Introduces new federal funding bans, limiting access to specific healthcare services for covered individuals.
  • Eligibility Verification Enhancements:
    Legislative: “States shall conduct quarterly eligibility reviews for all Medicaid beneficiaries.”
    Effect: Adds frequent review requirements, increasing administrative workload and risk of disenrollment due to paperwork or delays.

⚖️ Constitutional & Legal Considerations:

  • Equal Protection: Healthcare restrictions may face legal challenges under the Equal Protection Clause, particularly for transgender and reproductive-age patients.
  • Administrative Burden: Frequent eligibility reviews may strain state resources and disrupt access to care through procedural terminations.

Division E: Environmental & Energy Policy

Overview: This division rolls back numerous clean energy provisions and accelerates fossil fuel development, altering the trajectory of federal environmental strategy.

1. Repeal of Green Energy Incentives

Legislative: “Sections 45Q, 48E, and 179D of the Internal Revenue Code are repealed effective December 31, 2025.”

  • Section 45Q: Carbon capture tax credits
  • Section 48E: Investment tax credits for clean energy
  • Section 179D: Deduction for energy-efficient commercial buildings

Impact: Reduces support for solar, wind, and carbon capture; weakens core provisions of the Inflation Reduction Act.

2. Expansion of Fossil Fuel Leases

Legislative: “The Secretary of the Interior shall resume quarterly lease sales for oil and gas exploration on federal lands and offshore waters.”

Impact: Undoes Biden-era limitations, circumvents NEPA reviews, increases fossil fuel development, and invites legal challenges.

3. Revocation of Clean Power Plan Successors

Legislative: “EPA is prohibited from promulgating any rule substantially similar to the Clean Power Plan.”

Impact: Prevents new federal carbon emission rules for power plants; curtails EPA's authority under the Clean Air Act; may conflict with West Virginia v. EPA precedent.

⚖️ Constitutional & Legal Concerns:

  • Separation of Powers: Congress preemptively limiting future agency rules may conflict with the nondelegation doctrine.
  • States’ Rights: Broad federal override could undermine state-led environmental initiatives, raising 10th Amendment concerns.

Division G: Civil Rights & Social Policy

Overview: Major redefinition of federal civil rights, health access, free expression, and social policy funding. This section introduces sweeping restrictions and protections that affect a wide range of public programs, triggering substantial legal and cultural debate.

1. Federal Funding Restrictions on Gender-Affirming Care and Abortion Services

Legislative: “No funds appropriated under this Act or any other Act shall be used, directly or indirectly, to provide, promote, or subsidize gender transition procedures or abortion-related services...”

  • Affected: Medicaid, Title X, federally funded clinics (e.g. Planned Parenthood, LGBTQ+ care centers)
  • Implications: Eliminates public funding for services and referrals; pressures states to conform or resist; places operational strain on health providers
  • Constitutional Issues:
    • Equal Protection Clause (14th Amendment): Disparate treatment of transgender individuals
    • Right to Privacy: Parallels ongoing abortion challenges post-Dobbs
    • First Amendment: Gag rules may violate provider speech rights

2. Title IX Redefinition

Legislative: “‘Sex’ shall be defined as biological sex at birth...”

  • Affected: Trans student protections, school bathroom/sports policies, pronoun accommodations
  • Implications: Reverses Biden-era guidance; likely legal clashes with progressive state laws
  • Legal Flashpoints:
    • Bostock v. Clayton County (2020): Gender identity covered under sex discrimination (Title VII)
    • Potential contradiction between Title VII and Title IX interpretations

3. "Free Speech on Campus" Provision

Legislative: “No institution of higher education receiving federal funds shall enforce policies that limit... expression based on viewpoint.”

  • Affected: Campus protest rules, speaker bans, DEI training limits, hate speech policies
  • Implications: Expands viewpoint protections; may inhibit enforcement of anti-harassment measures
  • Constitutional Context: Strengthens First Amendment rights, but could undermine Title VI/IX obligations

4. Defunding of “Woke Education and CRT” Programs

Legislative: “No funds shall be allocated to curricula... that promote critical race theory or divisive concepts...”

  • Affected: DEI grants, inclusive history education, arts/cultural programming
  • Implications: Academic chilling effect; vague terms allow broad enforcement discretion
  • Legal Concerns: Due process violations for lack of legal clarity; inconsistent definitions of “CRT” and “divisive” concepts

5. Religious Freedom Expansion

Legislative: “No individual, business, or organization shall be compelled by federal law to provide services that conflict with sincerely held religious beliefs.”

  • Affected: Anti-discrimination enforcement in healthcare, education, public accommodations
  • Implications: Legal basis for denying services to LGBTQ+ individuals; overrides some state protections
  • Legal Flashpoints:
    • Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado
    • Fulton v. Philadelphia
    • Free Exercise Clause vs. Equal Protection Clause conflict

📅 Implementation Timeline

  • Immediate: Funding restrictions, CRT bans, religious freedom clause
  • 90 Days: Title IX redefinition enforcement via Dept. of Education
  • 180 Days: Colleges/universities must certify compliance with free speech provisions or risk grant suspension

🔥 Controversies

  • State Opposition: Governors and AGs from California, Massachusetts, Illinois, and New York have signaled intent to challenge provisions related to Title IX, healthcare restrictions, and DEI funding in federal court.
  • Civil Rights Lawsuits: Legal coalitions including the ACLU, Lambda Legal, and Planned Parenthood have publicly prepared litigation citing Equal Protection, First Amendment, and due process violations.
  • Academic and Medical Backlash: University presidents, public health associations, and the American Medical Association have issued statements condemning provisions they say restrict evidence-based care and academic freedom.
  • Religious Exemption Scope: The vague wording of “sincerely held beliefs” has raised fears of expanded discrimination in housing, education, and emergency services — including LGBTQ+ denial of care.
  • Federal-State Conflict Zones: Several states have passed conflicting laws protecting gender-affirming care, reproductive rights, and inclusive curricula, setting up likely supremacy clause showdowns.

Division H: Military & Defense Spending

Overview: Dramatically expands the defense budget, updates personnel policies, and shifts military strategy toward faster, tech-driven engagement with reduced congressional oversight. Framed as both a national security and economic stimulus initiative.

1. $150 Billion Defense Funding Increase

Legislative: “There is hereby authorized and appropriated an additional $150,000,000,000 for Department of Defense expenditures for fiscal year 2025.”

  • Breakdown: $45B UAVs, $30B nukes, $25B hypersonics, $20B Cyber Command, $18B contingency ops, $12B base upgrades
  • Implications: Largest annual defense expansion since 2001; boosts contractors; concerns over "black budget" transparency

2. Personnel Restructuring and Pay Reform

Legislative: “Base pay scales for E-1 to E-6 shall increase by 15%. STEM bonuses may exceed caps by 30%.”

  • Affected: Enlisted troops, STEM-qualified recruits
  • Implications: Enhances competitiveness in cyberwarfare fields, but may widen enlisted–officer income divide

3. War Powers Delegation Expansion

Legislative: “The President may authorize the deployment of armed forces in response to cyber threats or hybrid warfare actions... reported within 72 hours.”

  • Implications: Narrows 1973 War Powers limits; vague "hybrid warfare" clause expands executive latitude
  • Legal Red Flags: May violate Article I war-declaration powers and lack clarity under constitutional separation of powers

4. Foreign Aid Tied to Military Procurement

Legislative: “No foreign military aid shall be disbursed unless 85% of funds are used to purchase U.S.-manufactured defense products or services.”

  • Implications: Shifts foreign aid into a domestic subsidy; may strain joint procurement deals with allies

5. Base Realignment and Closures (BRAC) Reactivation

Legislative: “The Secretary of Defense shall initiate a new round of BRAC analysis for the 2026–2030 period...”

  • Implications: Could lead to base closures; likely to provoke local political backlash over job losses

📅 Implementation Timeline

  • FY2025: Funding increases and tech programs become active
  • January 2026: Personnel pay reforms begin
  • Immediately: Executive cyberwar deployment authority is in effect
  • FY2026: Foreign military aid conditions apply

⚖️ Constitutional & Legal Concerns

  • War Powers Clause (Art. I, §8): Delegating combat authority without conditions undermines Congress’s war declaration role
  • Appropriations Clause (Art. I, §9): Contingency funding may enable undeclared engagements without oversight
  • Due Process (U.S. personnel): AI-driven targeting implied in defense tech funding raises legal and ethical questions

🔥 Controversies

  • Executive Overreach: Legal scholars and civil liberties groups argue the cyberwar authority expansion bypasses democratic accountability
  • Unaccounted “Contingency” Funds: Critics point to vague spending in overseas operations as a potential blank check for off-book conflicts
  • Industry Influence: Watchdogs warn of growing influence of private defense contractors in shaping national policy and spending priorities
  • BRAC Fallout: May disproportionately impact rural or economically struggling regions reliant on military bases for jobs
  • Foreign Relations Risk: Procurement clause could weaken interoperability with NATO partners and allied nations using non-U.S. systems

Division I: Executive Branch & Federal Agency Reorganization

Overview: Restructures the federal bureaucracy to centralize regulatory power under the President. Dissolves key independent agencies, alters rulemaking procedures, limits inspector general independence, and advances the unitary executive theory.

1. Elimination of Select Independent Agencies

Legislative: “OFCCP, ACUS, and CFPB are hereby dissolved effective 180 days from enactment.”

  • Affected: Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB), Office of Federal Contract Compliance Programs (OFCCP), Administrative Conference of the U.S. (ACUS)
  • Implications: Ends federal enforcement of predatory lending protections, anti-discrimination in contracting, and administrative law reform
  • Legal Flags: SCOTUS review likely for CFPB due to existing challenges; Title VII enforcement gaps possible if OFCCP duties aren't reassigned

2. Consolidation of Rulemaking Authority

Legislative: “No federal agency shall issue or enforce regulations... exceeding $50 million without approval from the Executive Office of the President.”

  • Affected: EPA, FDA, OSHA, CFPB (prior to elimination), and others
  • Implications: Concentrates economic regulation approval in the White House; prioritizes political oversight over scientific review
  • Legal Red Flags: Nondelegation doctrine challenges likely; APA lawsuits may arise over procedural violations

3. "At Will" Presidential Removal of Civil Officers

Legislative: “The President may remove any principal officer of the U.S. at will...”

  • Targets: Federal Reserve, FTC, NLRB, and similar “independent” boards
  • Implications: Embeds unitary executive theory into statute; weakens agency insulation from political interference
  • Legal Red Flags: Defies Humphrey’s Executor precedent; raises Article II/Article I balance issues

4. Centralization of Inspector General Oversight

Legislative: “All Inspector General reports must be submitted to the White House Counsel before public or congressional release.”

  • Implications: Undermines whistleblower confidence; may suppress findings unfavorable to the administration
  • Legal Flags: Whistleblower Protection Act risks; possible First Amendment and oversight violations

5. Creation of the Office for Federal Efficiency and Deregulation (OFED)

Legislative: “Establishes OFED under the Executive Office... to eliminate duplicative regulations and fast-track executive orders.”

  • Implications: Centralized deregulation control without Senate-confirmed oversight; vague mandate allows sweeping authority
  • Risks: “Duplicative” undefined — broad interpretation may affect healthcare, labor, environment, and more

📅 Implementation Timeline

  • 180 Days: Agency dissolutions become final
  • Immediate: Regulatory review and officer removal authority active
  • 30 Days: IG reporting rerouted
  • 90 Days: OFED launched with $500M in funding

⚖️ Constitutional & Structural Concerns

  • Separation of Powers: Shifts regulatory balance from Congress and independent agencies to the Executive
  • Due Process: Reduces oversight transparency, especially around IG reports and public accountability
  • Vagueness & Overreach: Terms like “duplicative regulations” undefined — invites arbitrary enforcement by OFED

🔥 Controversies

  • CFPB Dissolution: Consumer groups and state AGs argue this removes one of the only major financial watchdogs protecting the public post-2008
  • Inspector General Restrictions: Bipartisan backlash over perceived political interference in oversight and retaliation risk for whistleblowers
  • Unitary Executive Theory: Legal scholars warn the provisions aggressively embed a theory previously rejected by multiple courts and traditions
  • OFED Criticism: Good governance watchdogs call OFED a “shadow agency” unaccountable to Congress or the public
  • Regulatory Chill Effect: Agencies may avoid proposing needed protections due to anticipated White House rejection or political cost

Division J: Technology, Artificial Intelligence & Digital Infrastructure

Overview: Sweeping legislation that centralizes AI oversight at the federal level, incentivizes tech innovation, expands broadband, and introduces a controversial federal digital ID system. Critics cite privacy, due process, and federalism concerns; supporters highlight modernization and efficiency.

1. Preemption of State-Level AI Regulation

Legislative: “No State... may enforce... any law... relating to artificial intelligence systems... for a period of 10 years.”

  • Effect: Nullifies state/local laws on AI governance
  • Concerns: Disempowers proactive states, deregulates AI development, opens litigation on 10th Amendment grounds

2. Federal Digital ID Program

Legislative: “Creation of a voluntary, encrypted Federal Digital Identity credential...”

  • Effect: Tied to biometrics; required for federal services like e-voting and FAFSA
  • Concerns: May evolve into de facto requirement; risks privacy abuses and digital exclusion of marginalized communities

3. AI Surveillance Expansion in Federal Agencies

Legislative: “All agencies shall... incorporate machine learning... into public safety and compliance enforcement.”

  • Effect: Mandates AI adoption in IRS, SSA, ICE, and more
  • Concerns: Potential algorithmic discrimination, lack of appeals, black-box decision-making in enforcement

4. Broadband Infrastructure & Deregulation

Legislative: “$60B... for broadband... States must repeal net neutrality rules and rate caps to receive funds.”

  • Effect: Expands rural access via funding tied to deregulation
  • Concerns: Coerces state policies; removes pricing controls; risks telecom monopoly consolidation

5. AI Research and Development (R&D) Incentives

Legislative: “35% refundable tax credit... up to $10M annually for AI-related R&D.”

  • Effect: Encourages private-sector AI growth
  • Concerns: Lacks ethical guardrails or open-source incentives; may deepen biased or harmful AI trends

📅 Implementation Timeline

  • AI regulation preemption: Immediate, 10-year sunset
  • Digital ID rollout: Begins Jan 2026; DMV integration by July 2026
  • Agency AI adoption plans: Due within 180 days
  • Broadband grants: Launch FY2026 Q1

⚖️ Summary of Legal and Ethical Concerns

Provision Concern
State AI regulation ban 10th Amendment violation, overbreadth, dormant commerce clause implications
Digital ID Privacy invasion, biometric data misuse, de facto mandatory ID
AI agency surveillance Due process erosion, lack of transparency or appeal rights
Broadband grants with preconditions Coercive federalism, weakened consumer protection
AI R&D tax credits No ethical standards, proprietary bias reinforcement

🔥 Controversies

  • Preemption Backlash: State attorneys general from California, Illinois, and Massachusetts have declared intent to challenge the AI regulation ban
  • Privacy Advocacy Pushback: ACLU, EFF, and other digital rights groups have warned the digital ID and AI surveillance sections represent “the foundation of a federal surveillance state”
